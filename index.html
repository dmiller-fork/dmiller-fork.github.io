Full Moon Constellations

The night sky is divided into 88 constellations. Taken all together, these constellations are called the celestial sphere. An observer can only see a portion of the celestial sphere depending on their location on Earth and the time of year. By observing which constellations are visible, you can figure out where you are on Earth and what time of year it is.

At any time during a calendar year, you can draw a ray originating from the sun that passes through the earth. This ray will point to one of 12 constellations. This constellation gives an astrological "month" of the year.

At night, it is impossible to see the sun, so the sun cannot be used as a reference point to draw the ray. However, on the night of a full moon, the sun, earth, and moon are all in a line. The location of the full moon in the night sky can be used as the "arrow" of the the ray.

Using these reference points, the location of the full moon within one of the 12 constellations allows us to determine the position of the earth in a solar year. From this information, you can define the seasons or make a rudimentary calendar. To perfom such calculations, you need a start date, called an "epoch" that defines a known point in the calendar. This application uses December 21, 2010 as the epoch date because that is the date of both a winter solstice and a full moon. From there, using the periodicity of the moon rotating around the earth, and the earth revolving around the sun, the positions of the astral bodies can be calculated for any time.

Our ancestors used the location of a full moon to know when to plant and harvest, but today, we just refer to these markers as seasons. The constellation that is closest to the full moon's position gives the season. Here is a chart for reference:

  • Winter: Gemini, Cancer, Leo
  • Spring: Virgo, Libra, Scorpio
  • Summer: Sagittarius, Capricorn, Aquarius
  • Autumn: Pisces, Aries, Taurus
