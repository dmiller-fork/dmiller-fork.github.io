Man's Search For Meaning

May 19, 2026 • Programming • 8 min read

I finished Man’s search for meaning earlier today. It provided many, many good insights on how to put problems into perspective. Essentially, an Auschwitz survivor meticulously explains how he maintained the will to live in the hardest of situations, and how he used the same pattern and belief to convince suicidal folks why they should choose to live. He is a clinical psychologist who applied his theory to great success. The theory is called “logotherapy”, where “logo” is the greek word for meaning.

The “meaning” that he thinks that others should strive for is not well defined because every person has to determine their own meaning or purpose. The book at one point gets a little meta, and states that a person can search for their own meaning as their meaning. I found this part at first contrite, but then upon further reflection slightly useful. Let me explain.

I grew up christian because my father was a christian methodist. I truly believed in God, and I was somewhat skilled at arguing the meaning behind certain verses in the bible. I remember being asked by a pastor when I was young, how I could justify when god lets a 99 year old man live, but a 1 year old dies. I simply replied that the 99 year old had something left to do. My father and the pastor did not like that answer. I was arrogant at the time, and to be honest, I’m still a little arrogant, but it feels less warranted as I grow older.

That is beside the point though. The Man’s search for meaning takes my point, and gives it depth. The 99 year old man can choose to continue living because he has the power to define his own meaning. Whether the meaning is to live on for a loved one or suffer through his old age with dignity or write some great manuscript, he gets to continue to live if he thinks his life has meaning. He can have meaning because he defines what his own meaning is.

The one year old’s death is pointless if you look at the death in a vacuum, but if you contextualize the death in terms of those that loved that one year old. Then the death of the child can be used to define the meaning of all of those individuals. Thus giving them meaning to live. Frankl gives many examples of “curing” people with phobias and obsessive compulsive disorder by essentially turning the problem on its head in a method he calls “paradoxical intention”, (see [page 124]). The idea is you shouldn’t commit suicide because your child died, you should live so his/her death is not pointless.

So how do you choose your own meaning? The question is not an easy one. The book only suggests that you read biographies of others that have found meaning, and see where these others have found meaning. Those people can provide guidance more so than people that haven’t found meaning.

There is an interesting question here though. Religion, at least christianity, can provide anyone meaning if they fully believe. The Bible is rife with verses that provide meaning: “those who believe in me will have everlasting life”. Other poignant passages include “I will make you fishers of men”, and “blessed are the poor and the sick and the needy for the first will be last and the last will be first in heaven.” I’m not quoting verses exactly here, but the Bible provides various paths by which a person can find meaning, either by looking forward to heaven or being a missionary and saving others. At first glance, a person could say, then you have proven the benefit of christianity. A more tenacious zealot might further argue that this proves the existence of god.

I however do not necessarily agree that christianity is good for people by giving them meaning. By giving people meaning, religion takes away the chance, nay the struggle, of one finding their own meaning. I don’t think finding meaning needs to be a necessarily unique experience, but the question, the struggle, and the ultimate solution are hopefully so useful and profound to the individual, simply removing that journey is robbing them of something wonderful.

I say this with a little fear because in the past few years, I have lost my religion. I no longer believe in god as a man in the sky who can hear my woes and help me if I pray hard enough. I feel severely weakened by this choice, but in a sense I have no choice once the evidence is laid before me. When I believed, I had a strong sense of meaning, but the number of lies I had to propagate ran counter to my beliefs in science.

Steve Jobs is a great example. He truly believed in his religion. It killed him, but he believed it to the end. I want my cake, and I want to be able to eat it too. I’d like to have a sense of meaning that didn’t kill me, but we shall see.

I feel like this deserves a little more introspection, so I’ll spend some time on it. One of the themes later in the book (section specifically about logotherapy) is how unhealthy American culture is by defining one’s value based on utility to the greater whole. His counter point is that he saw people do heroic things that don’t have short or long term utility. An example is when an SS soldier would tell a man to do something against his morals or be shot. A few people chose to be shot over breaking their morals. This is simply amazing. The loss of one life is likely bad from a utility standpoint. However, this sacrifice has value to the person who lost his life. The anecdote can be extended to choosing hard labor for a day without food over ratting out another prisoner who stole food. Again, this suffering has meaning, and therefore value to the one suffering.

The greater whole is not better off. From a prisoner’s dilemma standpoint, the thief, who is morally fine because they are just trying to survive in a concentration camp, has gained a little food, but everyone else is given no food. There are two calculations to consider, by keeping their mouth shut, the thief lives, but net food is less. So if two people die from starvation, then the Nash equilibrium and the true optimum dictate telling on the thief. Frankl says no. Frankl says the individual suffering of each person has its own value that cannot and should not be considered from a utilitarian standpoint.

As someone who is over forty and constantly compared to my peers in terms of production at a government agency, this philosophy/psychological stance could have great value to me. I can produce no net value and still live by some code that means I should keep living.

Toward the very end of the book, he sort of walks it back though. A person who can avoid suffering should. Therefore, suffering is not afforded as a sense of meaning to those born in privilege, unless the suffering is unavoidable.

This is hard to come to terms with. It means if I continue to slave away at my production based job, then I am suffering needlessly because I can find other work given the opportunity. However, the risk is great. The economy is bad. Given my history, I will likely limp along getting a few contract jobs here and there, while my wife works full time. I will become less and less of a utility based meaning person, and more and more of either a care-based meaning person or suffering based meaning person.

I’d much rather have a manuscript or company be my sense of meaning, but I cannot. I cannot. I cannot. I cannot. When I have kids and a wife who have daily struggles, my brain revolves entirely around them. At some point, my kids will grow up, and not need me anymore, but here today, I am a slave to them because it provides more purpose than a single company or manuscript ever could.

If I define my meaning by my family, how can I exist when I have so many ideas and thoughts that none of them care about. I love them, but I cannot stop my wanting to some day write a paper, or build a company that will change the world.

I guess I know one thing. I will never put my selfish and possibly delusional ideas of grandeur in front of my family. They will always be my priority. When I die, I want my oldest son to still love me. That is all.
